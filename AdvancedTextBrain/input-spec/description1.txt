Dr. Bellweather had scarcely removed his rain-darkened coat when Mrs. Finch placed the school committee's letter beside the cold teapot. The letter was mostly about the cracked plaster in the east corridor, the unfortunate disappearance of three brass coat hooks, and whether the caretaker had truly seen a fox near the bicycle shed. None of this, Bellweather observed, explained why the committee had summoned him.

The useful part appeared halfway down the second page. The school wished to hold a detective game during its autumn family evening. It was not to be a theatrical performance, though Mr. Graves repeatedly called it one, and it was not to be an escape room, despite the deputy head's enthusiasm for padlocks. The children were to investigate the fictional disappearance of a silver-coloured cardboard compass from the school library.

There would probably be twenty-four children, although an earlier note says twenty-six. Mrs. Finch later confirmed that the registration list contained twenty-four names and that this was the number to use. Their ages ranged from eight to ten. Parents might stand nearby, but the game itself was intended for the children. Four adults would supervise: the librarian, two teachers, and one parent volunteer.

The head teacher wanted the whole activity finished in forty-five minutes because a music recital would begin immediately afterward. Mr. Graves suggested an hour, but the timetable attached to the committee letter leaves only forty-five minutes. Setup may begin thirty minutes before the children arrive. Cleanup should take no more than fifteen minutes.

The game should use the library, the adjacent reading room, and the enclosed central courtyard. The basement, kitchen, staff offices, parking area, street, roof, and maintenance rooms are not to be used. No child should need to climb, crawl under furniture, move heavy objects, open electrical cabinets, use a real key, or leave the supervised area. Running is discouraged because the library floor becomes slippery in wet weather.

One child uses a wheelchair. Every clue, object, and route must be reachable without stairs and without requiring another child to lift or carry that participant. Another child reads slowly, so clues should use short sentences and familiar words. The committee would like the atmosphere to feel mysterious, but not frightening. There should be no blood, weapons, threats, ghosts, corpses, kidnapping, or realistic crime. A missing cardboard compass is considered sufficiently dramatic.

The story should resemble an old-fashioned investigation with observations, harmless suspects, and deductions. It should not use the names Sherlock Holmes, Dr. Watson, Professor Moriarty, or any other existing fictional character. The detective, suspects, school, and objects must have original names. The committee likes fog, coded notes, footprints, train timetables, and curious clocks, but these are ideas rather than mandatory elements.

Some discussion concerned the number of clues. The librarian proposed eight, a teacher proposed six, and the parent volunteer proposed ten. No final number was approved. The final specification must therefore identify the number of clues as an unresolved decision rather than quietly choosing one. The same is true of whether children work in four teams of six or six teams of four. Both arrangements appear in the notes, and neither was accepted.

The compass itself is not valuable and must not be made of metal or glass. It may be cardboard, foam board, or another lightweight material, but the exact construction is undecided. It must be large enough to see easily and must not have sharp edges. Any printed material should be producible on the school's ordinary colour printer. Other supplies should be obtainable from a normal stationery shop or dollar store. The total materials budget must not exceed seventy-five Canadian dollars.

The game should include a beginning explanation, an investigation period, and a final group solution. Children should have a way to record observations. The final answer must depend on evidence found during the activity, not on guessing the adult's favourite suspect. Every participating child should receive the same small prize or certificate at the end, regardless of which team finishes first. The committee does not want a single winning child.

Mrs. Finch added, almost as an afterthought, that one of the children has a severe peanut allergy. Food is not required for the game and should preferably not be used as a clue or prize. If any edible prize is proposed, the specification must identify allergy verification as unresolved because no approved food list exists. The safer interpretation is to use non-food prizes, but the source does not formally select the prize type.

The final document must describe the story premise, participants, spaces, timing, supervision, accessibility, clue rules, materials, setup, activity sequence, ending, safety restrictions, budget, unresolved decisions, and acceptance criteria. It must not write the actual full story or invent the final clue set unless a later instruction requests implementation.

Bellweather read the letter twice. Most of the pages concerned the coat hooks.
